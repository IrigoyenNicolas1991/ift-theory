Abstract

We present a field-theoretic formulation of the Intangible Field Theory (TCI), in which spacetime is permeated by an elastic medium (the I-medium) of equilibrium density \(\rho_0\) and equation of state \(P=\rho_0 c^2\). Material particles carry a conserved scalar charge \(T\) that repels the I-medium, producing localized density deficits (voids) described by a scalar vacancy field \(\phi\). From this single framework, we derive three results: (i) the rest energy of a particle is \(E=mc^2\), arising as the elastic potential energy stored in the deformed medium; (ii) the gravitational interaction between two particles obeys Newton's inverse-square law, \(F=Gm_1 m_2/r^2\), with \(G\) determined by the medium's parameters; (iii) the inertial mass equals the gravitational mass, with the energy of a moving void given exactly by \(E(v)=\gamma mc^2\). The equivalence of gravitational mass, inertial mass, and \(E/c^2\) is not postulated but follows from the fact that all three quantities measure the same physical property: the deficit of I-medium displaced by the charge \(T\). As a consistency check, we derive the gravitational redshift \(z=-\Delta\Phi/c^2\) from the vacancy field's effect on local clock rates, reproducing the general-relativistic result. Extending the analysis to second order in the medium's response, we show that a single curvature parameter of the equation of state, calibrated by the observed solar light deflection (\(\beta=4\)), yields the effective refractive index \(n=1+2GM/(c^2 r)\) of weak-field general relativity (PPN \(\gamma=1\)) and reproduces the Shapiro time delay with no further freedom. At second order, applying the triple equivalence locally upgrades the coupling to \(E=mc^2 e^{-\phi}\), and Mercury's perihelion (\(42.98''\)/century) then fixes the placement of the nonlinearity in the temporal kinetic sector (PPN \(\beta_{\rm PPN}=1\)), from which the vanishing Nordtvedt effect and the geodetic precession follow with no adjustable freedom. The minimal strong-field completion is the exponential (Yilmaz-type) metric, which departs from Schwarzschild at third order and predicts black-hole shadows \(4.6\%\) larger and ringdown frequencies \(4.4\%\) lower than general relativity — a correlated signature controlled by a single geometric quantity, consistent with current observations and decisively testable at next-generation precision. We also report an adverse result with full candor: gravitational-wave polarization measurements (Bayes factors \(\gt 10^{20}\) for pure tensor over pure vector, GW170817) falsify the theory's radiation sector in its linear-elastic form, since an isotropic elastic medium supports only scalar and vector wave modes; the required spin-2 completion is identified as the principal open problem. The formulation is Lorentz-covariant and reproduces the standard relativistic energy–momentum relation. We discuss the parameter space, connections to the observed vacuum energy density and the MOND acceleration scale, and outline open problems.

Keywords: modified gravity, scalar field theory, mass–energy equivalence, inertia, equivalence principle, vacuum energy, gravitational redshift, light deflection, Shapiro delay

1Introduction

Three conceptually distinct quantities in physics turn out to be experimentally identical: the gravitational mass \(m_g\) (the source and response to gravitational fields), the inertial mass \(m_i\) (the resistance to acceleration), and the rest energy \(E_0/c^2\) (the energy content of a body at rest divided by \(c^2\)). Their equality has been verified to extraordinary precision — \(|m_g-m_i|/m_i \lt 10^{-15}\) (Touboul et al., 2022) — yet no widely accepted explanation exists for why they should coincide.

Newton observed the proportionality of \(m_g\) and \(m_i\) empirically and verified it with pendulum experiments. Einstein elevated this coincidence to a principle — the Equivalence Principle — asserting that no local experiment can distinguish uniform acceleration from a gravitational field. The relation \(E_0=m_i c^2\) emerged later from the postulates of special relativity. These are descriptions, not explanations: they state that the quantities are equal, not why.

In this paper, we propose that the triple equality follows from a single physical mechanism. We introduce a scalar field theory in which:

  1. Spacetime is filled with an elastic medium (the I-medium) of equilibrium density \(\rho_0\) and equation of state \(P=\rho_0 c^2\).
  2. Material particles carry a scalar charge \(T\) that repels the I-medium, creating localized density deficits (voids).
  3. The vacancy field \(\phi(\mathbf{x},t)\), measuring the fractional deficit of I-medium, satisfies a sourced Klein–Gordon equation.

From these ingredients, the rest energy \(E=mc^2\), Newton's gravitational law, and the relativistic inertia \(E(v)=\gamma mc^2\) all emerge as consequences of a single quantity: the total I-medium displaced by the charge \(T\).

The paper is organized as follows. Section 2 states the postulates and defines the field theory. Section 3 derives \(E=mc^2\) from the elastic energy of the medium. Section 4 derives Newton's law of gravitation. Section 5 derives the relativistic energy–momentum relation and the equivalence of inertial and gravitational mass. Section 6 discusses the parameter space, connections to cosmological observables, and the MOND scale. Section 7 addresses the relationship to prior work, derives the gravitational redshift as a consistency check, and discusses open questions.

2Postulates and Field Equations

2.1The I-medium

Postulate 1 (I-medium). Space is permeated by a uniform elastic medium — the I-medium — with equilibrium mass density \(\rho_0\) and equation of state \[ P=\rho_0\,c^2, \tag{1} \] where \(c\) is the speed of propagation of small perturbations in the medium. The I-medium's constituents are mutually repulsive, which maintains the uniform density \(\rho_0\) in the absence of matter.

Equation (1) is the standard relation between pressure, density, and sound speed for an elastic medium: \(c_s=\sqrt{dP/d\rho}\), which for the linear form (1) gives \(c_s=c\). (For a general equation of state \(c_s=\sqrt{dP/d\rho}\ne\sqrt{P/\rho}\); the distinction becomes essential in Section 7.4, where the curvature of \(P(\rho)\) is shown to govern light deflection.) The identification of the propagation speed with the speed of light follows from the assumption that electromagnetic waves are perturbations of this medium.

Remark 2.1. The equation of state \(P=\rho c^2\) corresponds to a relativistic stiff fluid. It also arises naturally in the context of a scalar field with canonical kinetic term (Mukhanov, 2005).

2.2The T-charge and the vacancy field

Postulate 2 (T-charge). Material particles possess a conserved scalar charge \(T\) that repels the I-medium. The repulsive interaction decreases with distance as \(1/r^2\), creating a localized density deficit (void) around each particle. The charge \(T\) is proportional to the mass of the particle: \[ T=\frac{K\mu^2}{\rho_0}\,m, \tag{2} \] where \(K\) is the elastic rigidity of the vacancy field and \(\mu\) is an inverse length scale.
Definition 2.2 (Vacancy field). The vacancy field \(\phi(\mathbf{x},t)\) measures the fractional deficit of I-medium density at each point: \[ \phi(\mathbf{x},t)\equiv 1-\frac{\rho_I(\mathbf{x},t)}{\rho_0}, \tag{3} \] where \(\rho_I(\mathbf{x},t)\) is the local I-medium density. Thus \(\phi=0\) corresponds to undisturbed medium, and \(\phi=1\) corresponds to complete absence of I-medium (the core of a void).

2.3Lagrangian and field equation

The dynamics of the vacancy field are governed by the Lagrangian density

\[ \mathcal{L}=\frac{K}{2c^2}\left(\frac{\partial\phi}{\partial t}\right)^2-\frac{K}{2}\lvert\nabla\phi\rvert^2-\frac{\Lambda}{2}\phi^2+J(\mathbf{x},t)\,\phi, \tag{4} \]

where \(\Lambda=K\mu^2\) is the restoring potential coefficient and \(J(\mathbf{x},t)\) is the source density due to T-charges. For a collection of point particles,

\[ J(\mathbf{x},t)=\sum_i T_i\,\delta^3\!\big(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}_i(t)\big). \tag{5} \]

The Euler–Lagrange equation yields

\[ \frac{1}{c^2}\frac{\partial^2\phi}{\partial t^2}-\nabla^2\phi+\mu^2\phi=\frac{1}{K}\sum_i T_i\,\delta^3\!\big(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}_i(t)\big). \tag{6} \]

This is a sourced Klein–Gordon equation. The left-hand side is Lorentz-covariant; the source transforms as a scalar density under Lorentz boosts when the particle worldlines are properly parametrized.

Remark 2.3 (Units and dimensions). The field \(\phi\) is dimensionless. The rigidity \([K]=\mathrm{J/m}\), the mass parameter \([\mu]=\mathrm{m^{-1}}\), the charge \([T]=\mathrm{J}\), and the source \([J]=\mathrm{J/m^3}\).

2.4Static solution for a single particle

For a single particle of charge \(T\) at rest at the origin, the static field equation is

\[ -\nabla^2\phi+\mu^2\phi=\frac{T}{K}\,\delta^3(\mathbf{x}). \tag{7} \]

The solution with \(\phi\to 0\) as \(r\to\infty\) is the Yukawa profile

\[ \phi_0(r)=\frac{T}{4\pi K}\,\frac{e^{-\mu r}}{r}. \tag{8} \]

This describes a void centered at the particle: \(\phi\) is maximal near \(r=0\) (where the medium is most depleted) and decays exponentially on a length scale \(\mu^{-1}\).

2.5Mass as medium deficit

The mass of the particle is defined as the total I-medium displaced:

\[ m\equiv\rho_0\int\phi_0(\mathbf{x})\,d^3x. \tag{9} \]

Substituting the Yukawa solution (8):

\[ m=\rho_0\cdot\frac{T}{4\pi K}\int_0^\infty\frac{e^{-\mu r}}{r}\,4\pi r^2\,dr=\frac{\rho_0 T}{K}\int_0^\infty r\,e^{-\mu r}\,dr=\frac{\rho_0 T}{K\mu^2}. \tag{10} \]

This confirms the relation \(T=K\mu^2 m/\rho_0\) (Postulate 2) and establishes that the charge and mass are determined by the same physical quantity: the total displacement of I-medium.

3Rest Energy: Derivation of \(E=mc^2\)

3.1Thermodynamic argument

The most direct derivation of the rest energy uses the equation of state of the I-medium.

Theorem 3.1 (Rest energy). The energy required to create a void of mass \(m\) in the I-medium is \(E=mc^2\).
Proof. Creating a void requires displacing I-medium against its internal pressure. The work required to evacuate a volume \(V\) from a medium at pressure \(P\) is \[ W=P\cdot V. \tag{11} \] Using the equation of state \(P=\rho_0 c^2\) and the effective volume \(V_{\text{eff}}=m/\rho_0\): \[ E=\rho_0 c^2\cdot\frac{m}{\rho_0}=mc^2. \tag{12} \] ∎

This argument is independent of the detailed field profile — it depends only on the equation of state and the definition of mass as displaced medium.

3.2Field-theoretic argument: elastic potential energy

The same result can be obtained from the field energy, providing a consistency check.

The self-energy of the static field configuration is

\[ E_{\text{self}}=\tfrac{1}{2}\,T\,\phi_0(r_0), \tag{13} \]

where \(r_0\) is a short-distance cutoff (the core radius of the void) and we evaluate the regularized potential at the source. This is the standard result for the energy of a point source coupled to a scalar field, analogous to \(\tfrac{1}{2}q\Phi(0)\) in electrostatics.

Substituting \(\phi_0(r_0)\approx T/(4\pi K r_0)\) (for \(\mu r_0\ll 1\)) and \(T=K\mu^2 m/\rho_0\):

\[ E_{\text{self}}=\frac{K\mu^4 m^2}{8\pi\rho_0^2\,r_0}. \tag{14} \]

Requiring \(E_{\text{self}}=mc^2\) yields the consistency condition:

\[ K\mu^4 r_0^2=6\,\rho_0 c^2 \tag{15} \]

(using \(m=\rho_0\cdot\tfrac{4}{3}\pi r_0^3\) for a uniform core of radius \(r_0\)). This relates the four parameters \(K\), \(\mu\), \(\rho_0\), and \(r_0\).

3.3Annihilation interpretation

The energy \(E=mc^2\) has a direct physical interpretation. If the charge \(T\) were suddenly removed (annihilated), the void would collapse: the I-medium rushes inward, and the stored elastic energy is released as waves propagating at speed \(c\). These waves carry the total energy \(mc^2\).

Formally, for \(t\gt 0\) after the source vanishes, the field satisfies the free Klein–Gordon equation

\[ \frac{1}{c^2}\frac{\partial^2\phi}{\partial t^2}-\nabla^2\phi+\mu^2\phi=0 \tag{16} \]

with initial conditions \(\phi(\mathbf{x},0)=\phi_0(r)\) and \(\dot\phi(\mathbf{x},0)=0\). Energy conservation guarantees that the total energy of the outgoing wave packet equals \(E_{\text{self}}=mc^2\).

The factor \(c^2\) arises because it is simultaneously (a) the elastic modulus per unit density, \(B/\rho_0=c^2\), which sets the energy stored per unit displaced mass; and (b) the propagation speed at which that energy is released. These are not two coincidences but two aspects of the same property of the medium: its stiffness.

4Gravitational Interaction

4.1Two-body interaction energy

Consider two particles with charges \(T_1\) and \(T_2\) at positions \(\mathbf{x}_1\) and \(\mathbf{x}_2\), separated by \(R=|\mathbf{x}_1-\mathbf{x}_2|\gg r_0\). Each generates a Yukawa field; the total field is approximately \(\phi\approx\phi_1+\phi_2\) (superposition in the linearized regime).

The Hamiltonian density, including the source–field coupling, is

\[ \mathcal{H}=\frac{K}{2}\lvert\nabla\phi\rvert^2+\frac{\Lambda}{2}\phi^2-J\phi. \tag{17} \]

The interaction energy is the cross-term:

\[ E_{\text{int}}=-T_2\,\phi_1(\mathbf{x}_2)=-\frac{T_1 T_2}{4\pi K}\,\frac{e^{-\mu R}}{R}. \tag{18} \]

The negative sign arises from the source–field coupling \(-J\phi\) in the Hamiltonian: placing the second charge in a region where \(\phi_1\gt 0\) lowers the total energy. The interaction is attractive.

Remark 4.1. This sign analysis is the crux of the gravitational mechanism. Unlike electrostatics — where like charges repel because the field energy increases with superposition — here the coupling \(-T\phi\) ensures that placing a void in a region already depleted of I-medium is energetically favorable. Voids attract each other through the medium's pressure differential.

4.2Newton's law

In the gravitational regime, the range \(\mu^{-1}\) far exceeds the inter-particle separation: \(\mu R\ll 1\). Then \(e^{-\mu R}\approx 1\) and:

\[ E_{\text{int}}\approx-\frac{T_1 T_2}{4\pi K R}. \tag{19} \]

Substituting \(T_i=K\mu^2 m_i/\rho_0\):

\[ E_{\text{int}}=-\frac{K\mu^4}{4\pi\rho_0^2}\,\frac{m_1 m_2}{R}. \tag{20} \]

The force is

\[ F=-\frac{dE_{\text{int}}}{dR}=-\frac{K\mu^4}{4\pi\rho_0^2}\,\frac{m_1 m_2}{R^2}. \tag{21} \]

Comparing with Newton's law \(F=-Gm_1 m_2/R^2\), we identify

\[ G=\frac{K\mu^4}{4\pi\rho_0^2}. \tag{22} \]
Proposition 4.2 (Inverse-square law). The \(1/R^2\) dependence of the gravitational force is a direct consequence of the three-dimensional Laplacian: the Green's function of \(-\nabla^2+\mu^2\) in three spatial dimensions is the Yukawa potential \(e^{-\mu r}/(4\pi r)\), which reduces to the Coulomb form \(1/(4\pi r)\) for \(\mu r\ll 1\).

4.3Why gravity is always attractive

The attractive nature of gravity follows from the structure of the Hamiltonian (17). For positive charges \(T\gt 0\) (all matter repels the I-medium), the coupling \(-J\phi\) is negative wherever the field is positive. Since the Yukawa field of particle 1 is everywhere positive (\(\phi_1\gt 0\)), placing particle 2 at any location lowers the total energy. There is no gravitational repulsion in this framework.

4.4Relation between G and the rest energy condition

Using the consistency condition (15), \(K\mu^4=6\rho_0 c^2/r_0^2\), we can express \(G\) as:

\[ G=\frac{3c^2}{2\pi\rho_0 r_0^2}. \tag{23} \]

Equivalently,

\[ \rho_0=\frac{3c^2}{2\pi G r_0^2}. \tag{24} \]

Given \(G\) and \(r_0\) (or equivalently, given two of the four parameters \(K\), \(\mu\), \(\rho_0\), \(r_0\)), the remaining parameters are fully determined.

5Inertia: Derivation of \(E(v)=\gamma mc^2\)

5.1Moving void

Consider a charge \(T\) moving at constant velocity \(\mathbf{v}=v\,\hat{\mathbf{x}}\). The field equation in the laboratory frame is

\[ \frac{1}{c^2}\frac{\partial^2\phi}{\partial t^2}-\nabla^2\phi+\mu^2\phi=\frac{T}{K}\,\delta^3(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{v}t). \tag{25} \]

In the stationary regime (co-moving frame), the Fourier-space solution is

\[ \tilde\phi(\mathbf{k})=\frac{T/K}{k_\perp^2+k_\parallel^2/\gamma^2+\mu^2}, \tag{26} \]

where \(\gamma=(1-v^2/c^2)^{-1/2}\), \(k_\parallel\) is the wave number along the direction of motion, and \(k_\perp^2=k_y^2+k_z^2\). The profile is Lorentz-contracted by factor \(1/\gamma\) along the direction of motion.

5.2Self-energy of the moving void

The self-energy is given by \(E=\tfrac{1}{2}T\phi(\mathbf{x}_0)\), where \(\phi(\mathbf{x}_0)\) is the (regularized) field at the particle's position. In Fourier space:

\[ \phi(\mathbf{x}_0)=\int\frac{d^3k}{(2\pi)^3}\,\frac{T/K}{k_\perp^2+k_\parallel^2/\gamma^2+\mu^2}. \tag{27} \]
Theorem 5.1 (Relativistic energy of a void). \[ E(v)=\gamma\,mc^2. \tag{28} \]
Proof. Perform the change of variable \(k_\parallel'=k_\parallel/\gamma\) in Eq. (27). The Jacobian gives \(dk_\parallel=\gamma\,dk_\parallel'\), so \[ \phi(\mathbf{x}_0)=\gamma\int\frac{d^3k'}{(2\pi)^3}\,\frac{T/K}{k'^2+\mu^2}=\gamma\,\phi_0(0), \tag{29} \] where \(\phi_0(0)\) is the (regularized) field at the source for the particle at rest. Therefore, \[ E(v)=\tfrac{1}{2}T\phi(\mathbf{x}_0)=\gamma\cdot\tfrac{1}{2}T\phi_0(0)=\gamma E(0)=\gamma mc^2. \tag{30} \] ∎

The change of variable is valid because the integrand, combined with Lorentz-covariant regularization, is a function of the invariant \(k_\perp^2+k_\parallel^2/\gamma^2+\mu^2=k_\mu k^\mu+\mu^2\) (the four-dimensional invariant mass of the mode).

Remark 5.2 (Covariant regularization). The proof requires that the UV regularization respects Lorentz covariance. A hard cutoff \(|k|\lt\Lambda_{\text{UV}}\) breaks covariance and yields \(E(v)/E(0)\neq\gamma\) at high velocities. With covariant regularization (e.g., \(\exp[-(k_\mu k^\mu+\mu^2)/\Lambda^2]\)), numerical integration confirms \(E(v)/E(0)=\gamma\) to machine precision for all \(v\lt c\) (see Appendix A).

5.3Inertial mass

Expanding for \(v\ll c\):

\[ E(v)=\frac{mc^2}{\sqrt{1-v^2/c^2}}\approx mc^2+\tfrac{1}{2}mv^2+\tfrac{3}{8}\frac{mv^4}{c^2}+\cdots \tag{31} \]

The kinetic energy \(\tfrac{1}{2}mv^2\) involves the same mass \(m\) that appears in the rest energy and in the gravitational interaction.

The four-momentum of the void is

\[ p^\mu=(\gamma mc,\ \gamma m\mathbf{v}) \tag{32} \]

and Newton's second law in the non-relativistic limit is

\[ F=\frac{dp}{dt}=ma\qquad(v\ll c). \tag{33} \]

5.4Physical mechanism of inertia

The result \(E(v)=\gamma mc^2\) has a clear mechanical interpretation. When a void accelerates, the surrounding I-medium must reorganize to accommodate the changing configuration. This reorganization propagates at speed \(c\). At low velocities (\(v\ll c\)), the medium responds quasi-statically and the kinetic energy grows as \(\tfrac{1}{2}mv^2\). As \(v\to c\), the void approaches the propagation speed of the medium, the field becomes increasingly compressed (Lorentz contraction), and the self-energy diverges as \(\gamma mc^2\to\infty\).

Inertia is thus the elastic resistance of the I-medium to the reconfiguration required by a change in the void's velocity.

5.5The triple equivalence

We can now state the central result:

Theorem 5.3 (Triple equivalence). The gravitational mass, inertial mass, and rest energy of a void are determined by the same quantity: \[ m_{\text{grav}}=m_{\text{inert}}=\frac{E_0}{c^2}=\rho_0\int\phi\,d^3x. \tag{34} \]
Proof. (i) The gravitational mass enters through Eq. (20): \(E_{\text{int}}\propto m_1 m_2\), with \(m_i=\rho_0\int\phi_i\,d^3x\). (ii) The inertial mass enters through Eq. (28): \(E(v)=\gamma mc^2\) with the same \(m=\rho_0\int\phi_0\,d^3x\). (iii) The rest energy is \(E_0=mc^2\) from Theorem 3.1.
All three use the definition \(m=\rho_0\int\phi\,d^3x\). The equivalence is not a postulate but a consequence of the fact that there is only one physical property — the I-medium deficit — manifesting in three roles. ∎

6Parameter Space and Cosmological Connections

6.1Free parameters

The theory contains four parameters: \(K\) (elastic rigidity), \(\mu\) (inverse range), \(\rho_0\) (medium density), and \(r_0\) (core radius). The consistency condition (15) removes one degree of freedom, leaving three. Newton's constant (22) provides a second relation. The speed of light enters through the equation of state (1).

The physically meaningful independent parameters can be taken as \(\rho_0\) and \(r_0\) (with \(K\) and \(\mu\) determined by the consistency and Newton's constant equations).

6.2The MOND connection

In galactic dynamics, a characteristic acceleration scale \(a_0\approx 1.2\times 10^{-10}\ \mathrm{m/s^2}\) separates the Newtonian and anomalous regimes (Milgrom, 1983). In TCI, this scale emerges as

\[ a_0=c\sqrt{G\rho_0}, \tag{35} \]

yielding

\[ \rho_0=\frac{a_0^2}{c^2 G}\approx 2.4\times 10^{-27}\ \mathrm{kg/m^3}. \tag{36} \]

This is approximately one proton mass per cubic meter.

6.3Vacuum energy density

The energy density of the I-medium is

\[ u=\rho_0 c^2\approx 2.2\times 10^{-10}\ \mathrm{J/m^3}. \tag{37} \]

The observed cosmological vacuum energy density is \(u_{\text{obs}}\approx 5.6\times 10^{-10}\ \mathrm{J/m^3}\) (Planck Collaboration, 2020). The ratio is

\[ \frac{u}{u_{\text{obs}}}\approx 0.4, \tag{38} \]

an agreement to within a factor of \(\sim 2.5\).

Remark 6.1. Quantum field theory predicts \(u_{\text{QFT}}\sim 10^{113}\ \mathrm{J/m^3}\), a discrepancy of 120 orders of magnitude with observation. The TCI estimate, derived from a galactic observable (\(a_0\)), agrees with the cosmological vacuum energy to order of magnitude without fine-tuning.

6.4The cosmic coincidence

The Hubble constant \(H_0\approx 70\ \mathrm{km/s/Mpc}\) gives

\[ cH_0\approx 7\times 10^{-10}\ \mathrm{m/s^2}, \tag{39} \]

which is of the same order as \(a_0\). In TCI, both scales are connected through \(\rho_0\):

\[ a_0=c\sqrt{G\rho_0},\qquad cH_0\sim c\sqrt{G\rho_{\text{crit}}}, \tag{40} \]

where \(\rho_{\text{crit}}=3H_0^2/(8\pi G)\) is the critical density. The numerical proximity \(a_0\sim cH_0\) follows from \(\rho_0\sim\rho_{\text{crit}}\), which in TCI is not a coincidence but a reflection of the fact that the I-medium density sets both the galactic and cosmological scales.

7Discussion

7.1Relation to the classical aether

The I-medium is not a resurrection of the 19th-century luminiferous aether. Three critical differences apply:

  1. Matter does not move through the I-medium; it is a perturbation of the medium. There is no aether wind.
  2. The field equation (6) is Lorentz-covariant. Measurement instruments are themselves voids in the medium and undergo the same Lorentz contraction and time dilation, rendering the medium's rest frame locally undetectable.
  3. TCI reproduces special-relativistic effects (Lorentz factor, energy–momentum relation) rather than contradicting them.

7.2Relation to prior work

Several frameworks share conceptual elements with TCI:

Superfluid vacuum theory (Sinha et al., 1968; Zloshchastiev, 2011) models the vacuum as a superfluid. TCI differs in treating the medium as a classical elastic fluid with repulsive self-interaction, not a quantum condensate, and in the specific mechanism of voids-as-matter.

Emergent gravity (Verlinde, 2011) derives Newtonian gravity from entropic considerations on holographic screens. TCI provides a concrete mechanical substrate rather than an information-theoretic argument.

Mach's principle and various proposals for inertia as interaction with the vacuum (Haisch et al., 1994; Rueda and Haisch, 2005) share the motivation that inertia should have a physical origin. TCI provides a specific field-theoretic realization.

7.3Gravitational redshift as a consistency check

As a further test of the framework's internal consistency, we derive the gravitational redshift directly from the vacancy field. This derivation requires no additional postulates beyond those stated in Section 2.

The vacancy field as gravitational potential

The interaction energy between a test mass \(m\) and a source mass \(M\) (Eq. 18) can be written as

\[ E_{\text{int}}=-m c^2\,\phi_M(r), \tag{41} \]

where \(\phi_M(r)\) is the vacancy field generated by \(M\), evaluated at the position of the test mass. Comparing with the Newtonian form \(E_{\text{int}}=m\,\Phi(r)\), where \(\Phi(r)=-GM/r\) is the gravitational potential, we obtain the identification

\[ \phi(r)=-\frac{\Phi(r)}{c^2}=\frac{GM}{c^2 r}. \tag{42} \]

This holds in the gravitational regime (\(\mu r\ll 1\)) and establishes a direct bridge between the TCI vacancy field and the Newtonian potential: \(\phi\) is the dimensionless gravitational potential. Note that \(\phi\gt 0\) everywhere (the medium is depleted), while \(\Phi\lt 0\) for an attractive source, so the relation \(\phi=-\Phi/c^2\) is sign-consistent.

Local clock rate

A clock at position \(r\) from a mass \(M\) is itself a collection of voids embedded in a region where the I-medium density is \(\rho_I(r)=\rho_0(1-\phi(r))\). All internal processes of the clock — electromagnetic oscillations, atomic transitions — are perturbations of the local medium. The total energy of a clock of mass \(m_{\text{cl}}\) at position \(r\) is

\[ E(r)=m_{\text{cl}}c^2+m_{\text{cl}}\Phi(r)=m_{\text{cl}}c^2\big(1-\phi(r)\big). \tag{43} \]

Since the oscillation frequency of any bound system scales with its energy, the proper time interval \(d\tau\) at position \(r\) relates to the coordinate time \(dt\) by

\[ d\tau=\big(1-\phi(r)\big)dt=\left(1+\frac{\Phi(r)}{c^2}\right)dt. \tag{44} \]

Since \(\Phi\lt 0\) near a mass, \(d\tau\lt dt\): clocks run slower in deeper gravitational wells.

Derivation of the redshift

Consider a photon emitted at radial coordinate \(r_e\) (near the source mass) and received at \(r_r\gt r_e\). In a static vacancy field, the coordinate frequency of the photon is conserved along its trajectory (time-translation invariance). The locally measured frequency at any position \(r\) is

\[ \nu_{\text{local}}=\frac{\nu_{\text{coord}}}{1-\phi(r)}. \tag{45} \]

At emission: \(\nu_{\text{emit}}=\nu_{\text{coord}}/(1-\phi(r_e))\). At reception: \(\nu_{\text{rec}}=\nu_{\text{coord}}/(1-\phi(r_r))\). The ratio is

\[ \frac{\nu_{\text{rec}}}{\nu_{\text{emit}}}=\frac{1-\phi(r_e)}{1-\phi(r_r)}. \tag{46} \]

In the weak-field regime (\(\phi\ll 1\)), expanding to first order:

\[ z\equiv\frac{\nu_{\text{emit}}-\nu_{\text{rec}}}{\nu_{\text{emit}}}=\phi(r_e)-\phi(r_r)=-\frac{\Delta\Phi}{c^2}, \tag{47} \]

where \(\Delta\Phi=\Phi(r_e)-\Phi(r_r)\lt 0\). The received frequency is lower than the emitted frequency: the photon is redshifted. This is the standard general-relativistic result in the weak-field limit.

Connection to the Schwarzschild metric

Equation (44) is identical to the GR weak-field proper time, \(d\tau_{\text{GR}}=\sqrt{g_{00}}\,dt\approx(1+\Phi/c^2)\,dt\), with the identification

\[ g_{00}=(1-\phi)^2\approx 1-2\phi=1+\frac{2\Phi}{c^2}. \tag{48} \]

This suggests that the time–time component of the Schwarzschild metric emerges as an effective description of the I-medium's density profile. Extending this correspondence to the full metric tensor — including the spatial components and the strong-field regime — is an important direction for future work.

Experimental verification

The Pound–Rebka experiment (1960) measured the gravitational redshift of gamma rays over a height \(h=22.5\ \mathrm{m}\) in Earth's gravitational field. The predicted shift is \(z=gh/c^2\approx 2.46\times 10^{-15}\). In TCI, the vacancy field difference is \(\Delta\phi=gh/c^2\), yielding the same value. The measurement confirmed this to \(\sim 10\%\), with subsequent work by Pound and Snider (1965) reaching \(\sim 1\%\) precision.

For GPS satellites at altitude \(h\approx 20{,}200\ \mathrm{km}\), the gravitational blueshift (\(\approx +45.9\ \mu\mathrm{s/day}\)) and the special-relativistic time dilation (\(\approx -7.2\ \mu\mathrm{s/day}\), from the Lorentz factor derived in Section 5) combine to give a net offset of \(\approx +38.7\ \mu\mathrm{s/day}\) — exactly the correction applied in practice, and consistent with the TCI prediction.

7.4Light deflection and the Shapiro delay: the medium's nonlinear response

Why the linear theory cannot bend light

The framework of Section 2 must confront a classic difficulty of scalar theories of gravity head-on. The equation of state (1) is exactly linear in \(\rho\), so the propagation speed of small perturbations, \(c_s^2=dP/d\rho=c^2\), is independent of the local density. (Note that the general formula is \(c_s=\sqrt{dP/d\rho}\), not \(\sqrt{P/\rho}\); the two coincide only for a linear equation of state.) A light wave crossing the vacancy profile of the Sun therefore propagates at the same speed everywhere, and the strictly linear theory predicts zero refractive deflection — the same failure that ruled out Nordström's scalar gravity (1913) after the 1919 eclipse. Light bending in TCI can only arise from the nonlinear response of the I-medium.

Curvature of the equation of state

Postulate 1 fixes the pressure and stiffness of the medium at equilibrium, but is silent about its response beyond first order. We therefore write the equation of state as a Taylor expansion about \(\rho_0\), with \(\Delta\equiv(\rho-\rho_0)/\rho_0\):

\[ P(\rho)=\rho_0 c^2\left[1+\Delta+\tfrac{\beta}{2}\Delta^2+\mathcal{O}(\Delta^3)\right], \tag{49} \]

where \(\beta\) is a dimensionless curvature parameter. The first two Taylor coefficients are already fixed by earlier results: \(P(\rho_0)=\rho_0 c^2\) preserves the derivation of \(E=mc^2\) (Section 3, which uses only the equilibrium pressure), and \(dP/d\rho|_{\rho_0}=c^2\) preserves propagation at speed \(c\) in the unperturbed medium. In a region of static background deficit \(\Delta=-\phi(r)\), the local propagation speed becomes

\[ c_s^2(r)=\frac{dP}{d\rho}\bigg|_{\rho(r)}=c^2\left[1-\beta\,\phi(r)\right] \;\Longrightarrow\; c_s(r)\approx c\left(1-\tfrac{\beta}{2}\,\phi(r)\right). \tag{50} \]

Near a mass the medium is depleted (\(\phi>0\)), so light travels slower there and bends toward the mass — the correct sign.

Effective refractive index and deflection

Since light is a perturbation of the medium, it propagates at the local speed \(c_s(r)\). The medium acts as a graded-index lens with

\[ n(r)=\frac{c}{c_s(r)}\approx 1+\frac{\beta}{2}\,\phi(r)=1+\frac{\beta}{2}\frac{GM}{c^2 r}, \tag{51} \]

using the identification (42), which was independently anchored by the redshift analysis of Section 7.3. In the eikonal approximation, a ray with impact parameter \(b\) crossing an index profile \(n-1=A/r\) is deflected by \(\alpha=\int \nabla_{\!\perp} n\, dl = 2A/b\), hence

\[ \alpha=\frac{\beta\, GM}{c^2 b}. \tag{52} \]

The observed solar deflection, \(\alpha=4GM/(c^2 b)=1.75''\) for grazing incidence, fixes

\[ \beta=4 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad n(r)=1+\frac{2GM}{c^2 r}, \tag{53} \]

which is precisely the weak-field isotropic refractive index of general relativity (PPN \(\gamma=1\)). We emphasize the epistemic status of this result: \(\beta\) is calibrated by the eclipse observation, not predicted — just as \(G\) itself is calibrated by Cavendish-type experiments. The physical content of the theory lies in what follows once \(\beta\) is fixed.

The Shapiro delay as a parameter-free prediction

With \(\beta=4\) fixed, light traverses the solar vacancy profile at coordinate speed \(c_s(r)=c(1-2\phi)\) and accumulates an excess travel time

\[ \Delta t=\frac{1}{c}\int (n-1)\,dl=\frac{2GM}{c^3}\ln\frac{4 r_1 r_2}{b^2} \tag{54} \]

(one way, endpoints at \(r_1, r_2 \gg b\)). For a radar signal from Earth to Venus at superior conjunction with grazing incidence, the round-trip excess is \(\approx 240\ \mu\mathrm{s}\) — the effect measured by Shapiro (1968) and confirmed in its modern form by the Cassini experiment (Bertotti, Iess and Tortora, 2003) at the level \(\gamma-1=(2.1\pm 2.3)\times 10^{-5}\). This is a second observable governed by the same parameter, with no additional freedom. A third follows immediately: the index (53) is frequency-independent, so radio and optical light must deflect identically, as confirmed by VLBI.

Consistency with the redshift, and a constraint on the microscopic Lagrangian

Variable-propagation-speed theories typically die on the gravitational redshift: if clock rates also scaled with \(c_s\), the predicted redshift would be \(2\Delta\Phi/c^2\) — twice the Pound–Rebka value. TCI avoids this because the two effects have different origins. Clock rates (Eq. 44) follow from the interaction energy, which is first order in \(\phi\) and independent of \(\beta\), giving the factor-1 redshift; light propagation follows from \(dP/d\rho\), which is sensitive to \(\beta\), giving the factor-2 deflection. This reproduces exactly the \(g_{00}\)-versus-\(g_{rr}\) structure of the weak-field metric: the temporal part was fixed by the redshift (Section 7.3), the spatial part by \(\beta=4\).

At the field-theoretic level, \(\beta\neq 0\) requires nonlinearity in the derivative sector of the Lagrangian — the acoustic-metric structure familiar from analogue gravity (Unruh, 1981; Visser, 1998). A potential-type self-coupling \(g\phi^3\) would instead produce a dispersive index, \(n(\omega)-1\propto\omega^{-2}\), i.e. frequency-dependent deflection, which VLBI excludes. That observation forces the nonlinearity into the kinetic terms is a genuine structural constraint on the theory; the covariant form of the required coupling is an open problem, and the post-Newtonian consequences of the nonlinear sector — which determine where in the kinetic terms the nonlinearity must reside — are worked out in Section 7.5.

7.5The post-Newtonian sector: Mercury's perihelion

The stakes

The anomalous perihelion precession of Mercury, \(42.98\pm 0.04''\)/century, is the classic second-order test. In the parametrized post-Newtonian (PPN) framework the precession per orbit is

\[ \Delta\omega=\frac{2+2\gamma-\beta_{\rm PPN}}{3}\cdot\frac{6\pi GM}{a(1-e^2)c^2}, \tag{55} \]

where \(\gamma\) is fixed by the first-order spatial sector and \(\beta_{\rm PPN}\) by the second-order temporal sector, \(g_{00}=1-2\phi+2\beta_{\rm PPN}\phi^2+\ldots\) Section 7.4 already fixed \(\gamma=1\). General relativity has \(\beta_{\rm PPN}=1\), giving \(42.98''\)/century.

The stakes must be stated plainly. The identification \(g_{00}=(1-\phi)^2\) of Eq. (48) has \(\phi^2\) coefficient \(1\), i.e. \(\beta_{\rm PPN}=\tfrac12\), which by (55) yields \(50.1''\)/century — excluded by more than \(100\sigma\). The linear coupling (43), taken at face value beyond first order, falsifies the theory in the solar system. Two second-order effects, both internal to TCI, decide its fate.

The local form of the triple equivalence

Equation (43) couples the potential to the bare mass \(m\). But the central principle of this theory — the triple equivalence of Section 6 — asserts that the T-charge tracks the mass, and that the mass is the local energy content, \(m_{\rm loc}=E/c^2\). Applied at every depth of the potential well rather than only asymptotically, each increment of potential acts on the current energy, \(dE=-E\,d\phi\), which integrates to

\[ E(r)=mc^2\,e^{-\phi(r)}, \tag{56} \]

superseding (43), which is recovered at first order. The exponential is not an ansatz: it is the theory's own equivalence principle applied self-consistently. The clock-rate argument of Section 7.3 then gives

\[ g_{00}=e^{-2\phi}=1-2\phi+2\phi^2+\mathcal{O}(\phi^3), \tag{57} \]

which coincides with the Schwarzschild metric in isotropic coordinates through \(\mathcal{O}(\phi^2)\) — deviations appear only at \(\mathcal{O}(\phi^3)\), i.e. in the strong-field regime (see Section 7.7). The gravitational redshift becomes exactly \(z=e^{\Delta\phi}-1\), leaving the first-order results of Section 7.3 intact.

The second-order exterior field

Equation (57) gives \(\beta_{\rm PPN}=1\) only if \(\phi\) itself receives no second-order correction. Writing the static exterior field of the Sun as

\[ \phi=\phi_1+\sigma\,\phi_1^2,\qquad \phi_1\equiv\frac{GM}{c^2 r}, \tag{58} \]

the total \(\phi_1^2\) coefficient of \(g_{00}\) becomes \(2-2\sigma\), i.e. \(\beta_{\rm PPN}=1-\sigma\), and Mercury demands

\[ \sigma=0.000\pm 0.003. \tag{59} \]

The value of \(\sigma\) is determined by the same nonlinear sector introduced in Section 7.4. Consider the general kinetic Lagrangian of the medium in its rest frame,

\[ \mathcal{L}=\frac{K}{2c^2}\,\mathcal{G}(\phi)\,\dot\phi^2-\frac{K}{2}\,\mathcal{F}(\phi)\,|\nabla\phi|^2-\frac{\Lambda}{2}\phi^2+J\phi, \qquad \mathcal{F}=1+f_1\phi,\;\; \mathcal{G}=1+g_1\phi. \tag{60} \]

The optical sector of Section 7.4 fixes only the ratio: \(c_s^2=c^2\,\mathcal{F}/\mathcal{G}\), so \(f_1-g_1=-\beta=-4\). The static sector, by contrast, depends on \(\mathcal{F}\) alone: varying (60) for time-independent fields and solving the exterior equation to second order yields \(\sigma=-f_1/4\). Equation (59) then forces

\[ f_1=0,\qquad g_1=4:\qquad \mathcal{L}=\frac{K}{2c^2}\,(1+4\phi)\,\dot\phi^2-\frac{K}{2}|\nabla\phi|^2-\frac{\Lambda}{2}\phi^2+J\phi. \tag{61} \]

The medium's nonlinearity resides entirely in the temporal (inertial) kinetic term: the medium's inertia stiffens with depletion, while its elasticity remains linear. The alternative placements are not merely disfavored — putting the nonlinearity in the spatial sector (\(f_1=\mp 4\)) predicts \(57.3''\) or \(28.7''\)/century, excluded by hundreds of standard deviations. The data do not just permit this structure; they determine it.

Numerical verification

As an independent check on the algebra, we integrated the exact equatorial orbit equation \(u''=G'(u)/2\), \(G(u)=B\,(e^2/A-1)/\ell^2-u^2\), for Mercury's elements (\(a=5.7909\times 10^{10}\ \mathrm{m}\), \(e=0.2056\)) over 100 orbits with a fourth-order Runge–Kutta scheme, extracting the perihelion advance from successive apsides. The results: \(A=e^{-2\phi}\) gives \(42.98''\)/century; \(A=(1-\phi)^2\) gives \(50.15''\); \(\sigma=\pm 1\) give \(57.31''\) and \(28.66''\) — each matching the PPN formula (55) to four significant figures, and only the first matching observation.

What is calibrated and what is predicted

As in Section 7.4 we state the epistemic status plainly: Mercury calibrates the placement of the nonlinearity (\(f_1=0\)), just as the eclipse calibrated its magnitude (\(\beta=4\)). The reward is everything that follows for free. With \(\gamma=\beta_{\rm PPN}=1\) now fixed, TCI predicts a vanishing Nordtvedt effect, \(\eta=4\beta_{\rm PPN}-\gamma-3=0\) — the Earth and Moon, despite their different gravitational self-energies, fall identically toward the Sun — confirmed by lunar laser ranging at \(\eta=(-0.2\pm 1.1)\times 10^{-4}\) (Williams, Turyshev and Boggs, 2004); and the full general-relativistic geodetic (de Sitter) precession, confirmed by LLR and Gravity Probe B. In total: two calibrated numbers (the magnitude \(\beta=4\) and its temporal placement) against seven independent observables — light deflection, the Shapiro delay, the frequency-independence of both, the gravitational redshift, Mercury's perihelion, the Nordtvedt null result, and geodetic precession.

7.6Predictions and experimental signatures

The Yukawa form of the interaction (18) predicts a deviation from Newtonian gravity at distances comparable to \(\mu^{-1}\). If \(\mu^{-1}\) is cosmological (comparable to the Hubble radius), deviations appear only at galactic and cosmological scales, potentially connecting to the MOND phenomenology.

The theory predicts that the vacuum energy density should be of order \(\rho_0 c^2\), which is confirmed to within a factor of \(\sim 2.5\).

The strong-field completion

Solar-system data fix the medium's response only through the orders tested there: \(\mathcal{G}=1+4\phi\), \(\mathcal{F}=1\) (Eq. 61). To extrapolate to compact objects a completion of \(\mathcal{G}\) is required, and the theory itself suggests one: the same multiplicative compounding that produced \(E=mc^2e^{-\phi}\) (Eq. 56) — each increment of depletion acting on the current response rather than the bare one — applied to the medium's inertial sector gives \(\mathcal{G}=e^{4\phi}\), \(\mathcal{F}=1\). The resulting effective metric is

\[ ds^2=e^{-2\phi}c^2dt^2-e^{+2\phi}\left(dr^2+r^2d\Omega^2\right),\qquad \phi=\frac{GM}{c^2 r}, \tag{62} \]

the exponential metric first studied by Yilmaz (1958), here obtained from a concrete physical mechanism. It agrees with the Schwarzschild metric (isotropic form) through \(\mathcal{O}(\phi^2)\) — leaving every solar-system result of Sections 7.3–7.5 intact — and departs at \(\mathcal{O}(\phi^3)\). We state the epistemic status plainly: the exponential completion is the minimal one consistent with the theory's own compounding principle, not a consequence of existing data; alternative completions would alter the strong-field numbers below without touching the solar system. Structurally, (62) has no event horizon: \(g_{00}>0\) everywhere, with a redshift factor \(e^{\phi}\) that grows superexponentially as \(r\to 0\) — infalling matter fades to black rather than crossing a horizon.

Black-hole shadows: a falsifiable divergence from general relativity

Light in the medium follows Fermat's principle with index \(n=\sqrt{B/A}=e^{2\phi}\). The photon sphere, \(d[r\,n(r)]/dr=0\), sits at \(r_{\rm ph}=2GM/c^2\) (isotropic), with critical impact parameter

\[ b_c^{\rm TCI}=2e\,\frac{GM}{c^2}\approx 5.4366\,\frac{GM}{c^2} \qquad\text{vs.}\qquad b_c^{\rm GR}=3\sqrt{3}\,\frac{GM}{c^2}\approx 5.1962\,\frac{GM}{c^2}, \tag{63} \] \[ \frac{b_c^{\rm TCI}}{b_c^{\rm GR}}=\frac{2e}{3\sqrt{3}}=1.0463: \tag{64} \]

TCI predicts black-hole shadows \(4.6\%\) larger than general relativity. The Event Horizon Telescope constrains the shadow sizes of M87* (2019) and Sgr A* (2022) to \(\sim\pm 10\%\) of the GR expectation, so the prediction is consistent with current data and decisively testable at the \(\sim 1\%\) precision projected for the next-generation EHT. A second independent discriminator comes from the innermost stable circular orbit: numerically, the ISCO orbital frequency in (62) is \(GM\Omega_{\rm isco}/c^3=0.06333\), \(6.9\%\) lower than the GR value \(6^{-3/2}=0.06804\), shifting the inner edge of accretion disks and the associated quasi-periodic oscillations. (Both numbers were verified by direct null-ray and timelike-orbit integration, with the same code reproducing the exact GR values \(3\sqrt3\) and \(6^{-3/2}\) as a control.) At the orbit of the star S2 around Sgr A* (\(\phi\sim 10^{-4}\)), by contrast, (62) is indistinguishable from GR, consistent with the GRAVITY measurement of the Schwarzschild precession.

Ringdown: the correlated signature

The remnant of a compact-object merger rings down at quasinormal frequencies that, in the eikonal approximation, are read off the light ring (Cardoso et al., 2009): \(\omega\approx\ell\,\Omega_c-i(n+\tfrac12)\lambda_L\), with \(\Omega_c=c/b_c\) the light-ring orbital frequency and \(\lambda_L\) its Lyapunov exponent. Numerically, both metrics satisfy \(\lambda_L=\Omega_c\) exactly — the Schwarzschild relation survives in the exponential metric — so both the ringdown frequency and the damping rate scale as \(1/b_c\):

\[ \frac{f_{\rm ring}^{\rm TCI}}{f_{\rm ring}^{\rm GR}}=\frac{\lambda_L^{\rm TCI}}{\lambda_L^{\rm GR}}=\frac{3\sqrt3}{2e}=0.956. \tag{65} \]

TCI therefore predicts ringdown frequencies and damping rates \(4.4\%\) lower than general relativity for the same remnant mass — exactly anticorrelated with the \(4.6\%\) larger shadow, since a single geometric quantity (\(b_c\)) controls all three strong-field observables. For a GW150914-like \(62\,M_\odot\) remnant this means \(\sim 192\) Hz versus \(\sim 201\) Hz (eikonal \(\ell=2\); absolute eikonal values carry \(\sim 15\%\) systematics, but theory-to-theory ratios are robust). Current inspiral–merger–ringdown consistency tests reach \(\sim 10\!-\!20\%\), so the prediction survives today and is testable by third-generation detectors (\(\sim 1\%\)). Caveats: the metric (62) is non-rotating, while astrophysical remnants spin, and a full confrontation requires the complete waveform, not the ringdown alone.

The radiation sector: a no-go result and the inertial channel

The polarization content of gravitational waves is decisive. LIGO–Virgo observations strongly favor two transverse tensor (spin-2) polarizations over pure scalar or pure vector alternatives. Here the naive TCI radiation sector fails, and it fails structurally. If voids couple to the medium only through the isotropic density deficit (the T-charge of Postulate 2), every source is a center of dilatation, with equivalent body force \(\mathbf{f}\propto\nabla\delta^3(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}_s(t))\) — longitudinal in Fourier space for every \(\mathbf{k}\), even for moving sources. By Helmholtz decomposition, no superposition of such sources excites transverse waves: a binary would radiate purely longitudinal (scalar) waves, in strong tension with the polarization tests. We state this as a no-go theorem for the density-only coupling.

The escape is already contained in the theory. Inertia in TCI is added mass (Section 5): an accelerating void exerts a reaction body force on the medium — precisely the vector coupling the no-go theorem requires. For a circular binary the two reaction forces are equal, opposite, and applied at opposite positions: zero net force, zero net torque, leaving a rotating force quadrupole with moment tensor \(M_{ij}\propto r_i r_j\), which rotates at \(2\Omega\) — twice the orbital frequency. In an elastically solid medium this radiates transverse shear waves at \(2\Omega\) with quadrupolar structure: the observed gravitational-wave phenomenology, produced by the same mechanism that already accounted for inertia. Reciprocally, interferometer mirrors respond to the velocity field of a passing shear wave through the same added-mass coupling. Two requirements follow: the I-medium must support shear (an elastic solid, not a fluid), and the multi-messenger bound \(|c_{gw}/c-1|\lt 10^{-15}\) (GW170817) fixes its shear modulus at \(\mu_s=\rho_0 c^2\), in exact parallel with the compressional sector \(P=\rho_0 c^2\).

Two calculations carry the theory's sharpest risk: (i) the angular response patterns of the shear channel versus the measured spin-2 antenna patterns; and (ii) the total binary luminosity versus the Hulse–Taylor orbital decay (\(\sim 0.2\%\)).

The first computation has now been carried out, and we report an adverse result with the same candor as the favorable ones. The differential response of an interferometer to a plane shear wave is governed by the symmetrized gradient of the displacement field, \(R_{ij}\propto\tfrac12(\varepsilon_i n_j+\varepsilon_j n_i)\) — the basis tensor of the vector polarization modes in the standard six-polarization decomposition; the compressional channel likewise yields the scalar modes. This is not an artifact of the coupling: in an isotropic elastic medium every linear wave decomposes into P \(\oplus\) S, and no response tensor built linearly from one transverse polarization vector and one propagation direction can reproduce the pure spin-2 structure of general relativity. The observations are decisive: GW170817, with its source position fixed by the electromagnetic counterpart, yields Bayes factors exceeding \(10^{20}\) in favor of pure tensor polarization over pure vector (stronger still over scalar), and GWTC-3 finds no evidence for non-tensorial modes. The radiation sector of TCI in its linear-elastic form is therefore falsified.

The static, optical, post-Newtonian and strong-field sectors (Sections 3–7.6 above) do not depend on the radiation mechanism and are unaffected. But a medium theory can accommodate the polarization data only by acquiring a rank-2 (quadrupolar) internal order parameter whose oscillations are genuine spin-2 waves — major structural surgery, not a patch, recorded as such among the open questions. We also note the deeper reading: the data demand a dynamical spin-2 field, and consistent self-interacting spin-2 theories are known to converge to general relativity. The lasting contribution of the I-medium picture may be to explain the static, optical and orbital sectors from a physical substrate while clarifying why the radiative sector must be Einsteinian.

7.7Open questions

Several important issues remain:

  1. Strong-field phenomenology beyond the shadow and ISCO: Section 7.6 quantifies the two cleanest discriminators (\(+4.6\%\) shadows, \(-6.9\%\) ISCO frequency). Remaining: the ringdown spectrum of merging compact objects (perturbation theory on the exponential metric (62)), and the accretion phenomenology of horizonless ultracompact objects.
  2. The spin-2 completion (the falsified sector): Section 7.6 records the falsification of the linear-elastic radiation sector by the LIGO–Virgo polarization measurements. A viable radiation sector requires the medium to carry a rank-2 (quadrupolar) internal order parameter whose oscillations are genuine spin-2 waves; whether such a completion exists without spoiling the successful sectors is now the theory's principal open problem.
  3. Covariant form of the nonlinear coupling: Sections 7.4–7.5 determine the nonlinearity's magnitude (\(\beta=4\)) and placement (the temporal kinetic term, Eq. 61) in the medium's rest frame; its explicit Lorentz-covariant completion remains to be constructed.
  4. Nuclear structure: How do protons, neutrons, and other particles emerge within this framework?
  5. Quantum regime: The connection between the classical field theory presented here and quantum mechanics.
  6. Composition of the I-medium: What are the microscopic constituents of the medium, and what determines \(\rho_0\)?

8Conclusion

We have presented a field-theoretic formulation of the Intangible Field Theory in which a single elastic medium, characterized by density \(\rho_0\) and equation of state \(P=\rho_0 c^2\), gives rise to three fundamental aspects of mechanics:

  1. Rest energy: \(E=mc^2\) is the elastic energy stored in the density gradient surrounding a void (Section 3).
  2. Gravity: \(F=Gm_1 m_2/r^2\) arises from the overlap of Yukawa fields generated by two T-charges, with \(G=K\mu^4/(4\pi\rho_0^2)\) (Section 4).
  3. Inertia: \(E(v)=\gamma mc^2\) follows from the Lorentz-covariant self-energy of the moving void (Section 5).

The triple equivalence \(m_{\text{grav}}=m_{\text{inert}}=E_0/c^2\) is not postulated but derived: all three quantities measure the total I-medium displaced by the particle's T-charge.

As a consistency check, the gravitational redshift \(z=-\Delta\Phi/c^2\) was derived from the vacancy field's effect on local clock rates (Section 7.3), reproducing the general-relativistic result and providing a bridge to the Schwarzschild metric through the identification \(g_{00}\approx(1-\phi)^2\). The spatial counterpart of that bridge was established in Section 7.4: a single curvature parameter of the equation of state, calibrated by the 1919 eclipse (\(\beta=4\)), yields the weak-field refractive index \(n=1+2GM/(c^2 r)\) and hence the full \(1.75''\) solar deflection, the Shapiro time delay, and the frequency-independence of light bending — the last two with no adjustable freedom. The post-Newtonian sector (Section 7.5) completed this picture: the triple equivalence applied locally gives \(g_{00}=e^{-2\phi}\), Mercury's perihelion fixes the nonlinearity's placement in the temporal kinetic term (Eq. 61), and the Nordtvedt null result and geodetic precession then follow parameter-free — in total, two calibrated numbers against seven independent solar-system observables. Beyond the solar system, the theory's minimal completion predicts a specific, falsifiable divergence from general relativity: black-hole shadows \(4.6\%\) larger and ISCO frequencies \(6.9\%\) lower (Section 7.6), within reach of next-generation event-horizon imaging. In the same section we record, with equal weight, the theory's first falsified sector: the polarization content of the observed gravitational waves (pure spin-2) cannot be produced by the linear waves of an isotropic elastic medium, which are exhaustively scalar and vector. The successful sectors are unaffected, but a viable radiation sector demands a spin-2 completion — the principal open problem, and possibly the deepest lesson of the medium picture.

The theory's parameter space is tightly constrained. A single density \(\rho_0\approx 2.4\times 10^{-27}\ \mathrm{kg/m^3}\) — determined by the galactic acceleration scale \(a_0\) — correctly predicts the order of magnitude of the cosmological vacuum energy and connects the galactic (MOND) and cosmological (Hubble) scales.

Whether this framework can be extended to reproduce the full phenomenology of general relativity (perihelion precession, gravitational waveforms, black holes) and quantum mechanics remains an open and important question. The results presented here — including the recovery of gravitational time dilation and the first-order optical sector — suggest that the attempt is worth pursuing.
